Financial Terms Used in This Report — Plain English Guide

Every term you'll see below, explained with IndiaMART's actual numbers as examples.

CMPCurrent Market Price — what one share costs right now on the stock exchange. IndiaMART's CMP is ₹1,970. This changes every second during market hours.
Market CapMarket Capitalization — the total value of ALL shares of the company. It's calculated as: CMP × Total Shares Outstanding. IndiaMART: ₹1,970 × 6.0 Cr shares = ₹11,837 Cr. Think of it as "how much would it cost to buy the entire company at today's price?"
EPSEarnings Per Share — how much profit the company made for each share. Calculated as: Net Profit / Total Shares. IndiaMART earned ₹551 Cr profit in FY25 with 6.0 Cr shares, so EPS = ₹551 Cr / 6.0 Cr shares = ~₹92/share. If you own 1 share, the company earned ₹92 "for you" last year.
P/E RatioPrice-to-Earnings — how many years of current earnings you're paying for when you buy the stock. Calculated as: CMP / EPS. IndiaMART: ₹1,970 / ₹92 = 19.6x. This means you're paying ₹19.6 for every ₹1 the company earns per year. Lower P/E = cheaper. A P/E of 50x means you're paying 50 years' worth of earnings — expensive! IndiaMART's 19.6x is the cheapest it has EVER been.
ATLAll-Time Low — the lowest value this metric has ever reached in the stock's history. IndiaMART's P/E is at ATL, meaning the stock has never been this cheap relative to its earnings since it listed on the stock exchange in 2019.
PercentileTells you where a value sits in the historical range. 0th percentile = lowest ever. 50th = exactly in the middle. 100th = highest ever. IndiaMART's P/E is at the 0th percentile — literally the cheapest point in 323 weekly observations.
P/B RatioPrice-to-Book — compares the stock price to the company's "book value" (total assets minus total liabilities, divided by shares). If P/B = 1, you're paying exactly what the company's assets are worth on paper. IndiaMART's P/B of 5.06x means you pay ₹5 for every ₹1 of net assets — normal for tech companies that have valuable brands and customers not captured in accounting books.
EV/EBITDAEnterprise Value / Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation. EV = Market Cap + Debt - Cash. It's like P/E but better because it adjusts for how much cash/debt the company has. IndiaMART has ₹3,051 Cr cash and only ₹26 Cr debt, so its EV is much lower than its Market Cap — making the business even cheaper than P/E suggests.
EBITDAEarnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation — a measure of "operating cash profit." It strips out accounting items (depreciation, amortisation) and financing items (interest, tax) to show how much cash the core business generates. Think of it as "how much money does the actual business make before the accountants and tax people get involved?"
Gross MarginThe percentage of revenue left after paying the direct cost of delivering the service. IndiaMART's 55% gross margin means: for every ₹100 of revenue, ₹55 is left after paying for servers, bandwidth, and direct costs. Higher = better pricing power.
Operating Margin (OPM)The percentage left after paying ALL operating costs (salaries, rent, marketing, etc.). IndiaMART's 31.6% OPM means: for every ₹100 of revenue, ₹31.6 is operating profit. This is the best measure of "how profitable is the core business?"
Net MarginThe FINAL percentage left after everything — operating costs, interest, taxes, other income. IndiaMART's 39.8% net margin is actually higher than operating margin because the company earns ~₹135 Cr/quarter in interest on its ₹3,051 Cr cash pile. That "other income" pushes net profit above operating profit.
ROCEReturn on Capital Employed — how efficiently the company uses its capital to generate profit. ROCE = Operating Profit / Capital Employed. IndiaMART's 34% ROCE means every ₹100 of capital invested in the business generates ₹34 of operating profit. Above 20% is excellent. Above 30% is exceptional. It tells you: "Is this company a good steward of the money invested in it?"
ROEReturn on Equity — similar to ROCE but only looks at shareholders' money (not debt). ROE = Net Profit / Shareholder Equity. It answers: "For every ₹100 shareholders have invested, how much profit did the company make?"
D/E RatioDebt-to-Equity — how much the company has borrowed relative to what shareholders own. IndiaMART's 0.011 means it has almost ZERO debt. For every ₹100 of shareholder money, only ₹1.1 is borrowed. Below 0.5 = conservative. Above 1.0 = aggressive. Above 2.0 = risky.
Current RatioCan the company pay its bills due in the next 12 months? Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities. IndiaMART's 2.39x means it has ₹2.39 of short-term assets for every ₹1 of short-term obligations. Above 1.5 = comfortable. Below 1.0 = potential cash crunch.
Net CashTotal Cash minus Total Debt. IndiaMART: ₹2,893 Cr cash - ₹26 Cr debt = ₹2,868 Cr net cash. This is money sitting in the bank that belongs to shareholders. It's like a safety cushion — even if the business has a terrible year, this cash exists.
OCF / CFOOperating Cash Flow / Cash Flow from Operations — actual cash generated by running the business (not accounting profit, but real money coming in). IndiaMART's OCF is ₹675 Cr vs Net Profit of ₹551 Cr. When OCF > Net Profit, earnings are "real" — the company is actually collecting more cash than it reports as profit. When OCF < Net Profit, be cautious — profits may be accounting entries, not real cash.
FCFFree Cash Flow — cash left after the company spends on maintaining/growing its assets (capex). FCF = CFO - Capex. This is the "true" cash available to return to shareholders via dividends/buybacks or reinvest in growth.
BetaHow much the stock moves relative to the overall market (Nifty). Beta = 1.0 means it moves exactly with the market. IndiaMART's beta of 0.20 means if Nifty falls 10%, IndiaMART typically falls only 2%. Low beta = less volatile, less risky, but also less upside in bull markets.
Deferred RevenueCash the company has already collected from customers but hasn't yet "earned" in accounting terms. IndiaMART collects annual subscriptions upfront — if a customer pays ₹60,000 for a yearly plan in January, only ₹5,000 is "revenue" in January (1/12th). The remaining ₹55,000 sits as deferred revenue. IndiaMART's deferred revenue of ₹1,775 Cr is essentially "guaranteed future revenue" — they already have the cash.
BVPSBook Value Per Share — the company's net assets (assets minus liabilities) divided by total shares. IndiaMART's BVPS is ₹390. The stock trades at ₹1,970, which is 5x book value — this premium reflects the value of IndiaMART's brand, customer relationships, and platform that don't show up on the balance sheet.
Dividend YieldAnnual dividend paid per share as a percentage of stock price. IndiaMART's 1.5% means if you buy at ₹1,970, you'll receive ~₹30/year in dividends. Not high, because IndiaMART prefers returning cash through buybacks and reinvesting in Busy Infotech.
Working Capital DaysHow many days of revenue are tied up in the business operations (receivables, inventory, minus payables). IndiaMART's -305 days is extremely unusual — it's negative because they collect cash upfront (deferred revenue) before delivering the service. Most companies have positive working capital days (they deliver first, get paid later). Negative = the company is funded by its customers, not by its own capital.
This Stock is at Its Cheapest-Ever Valuation

What does "cheapest ever" mean? We measure cheapness using P/E (Price-to-Earnings). IndiaMART's P/E is 19.6x today. Over the last 6 years (323 weekly observations since its IPO in 2019), the P/E has ranged from 19.6x (now) to 199x (during the 2021 bubble). The median (middle value) was 48.9x. Today's 19.6x is the absolute lowest — the 0th percentile.
Putting it in rupees: At the median P/E of 48.9x and current EPS of ~₹92, the stock "should" trade at 48.9 × ₹92 = ₹4,497. It actually trades at ₹1,970 — a 56% discount to its historical median valuation. Either the market is right that growth is over, or this is a significant mispricing.
Why is it cheap? Two reasons: (1) paying subscriber growth has stalled at ~220K — investors worry the company can't grow its customer base anymore, and (2) quarterly net profit is lumpy because of ₹3,051 Cr in treasury investments that cause large mark-to-market swings in "other income."

The counterargument: Revenue is still growing 13% (from ARPU increases), earnings grew 55% last year, the company has ₹2,868 Cr net cash (24% of its entire market cap), and Busy Infotech (accounting software subsidiary) is growing 50%+ per year.

Key Themes — Last 4 Quarters

1. Subscriber Growth Has Stalled — Paying suppliers peaked at 222K (Q2 FY26) and declined to 221K in Q3 — the first negative quarter. This is the #1 concern for investors. IndiaMART has ~1.5% penetration of 15-20M GST-registered suppliers in India — huge TAM (Total Addressable Market) — but organic growth has plateaued. The silver tier (cheapest plan, ~₹30K/year) churns at ~7% per month, meaning ~50% of silver customers leave each year. Gold/platinum churn is only ~1%. (Q3 FY26 concall p.6)

2. ARPU Is the Only Growth Lever Right Now — Since they can't grow subscribers, all revenue growth comes from getting existing customers to pay more. ARPU: ₹62K → ₹64K → ₹65K → ₹67K over 4 quarters. This 6-7% YoY growth is driven by pricing hikes, multi-city products, and customers upgrading from silver to gold/platinum. Management says 6-8% ARPU CAGR is sustainable long-term. (Q3 deck p.22)

3. Busy Infotech Is the Hidden Growth Engine — This accounting software subsidiary (like Tally) is growing 50%+ per year. Revenue ₹32 Cr/quarter, deferred revenue ₹112 Cr (up 56%), 12,000 new licenses every quarter. IndiaMART has invested ₹725 Cr total in the accounting software space (Busy, Vyapar, RealBooks). The thesis: IndiaMART's 220K paying SME customers are a captive distribution channel for accounting/ERP software. (Q3 deck p.8, Q2 concall p.3)

4. AI Is Both a Threat and an Opportunity — CEO has been remarkably honest about AI's dual impact. Threat: AI crawlers (ChatGPT, Grok, DeepSeek) are scraping supplier catalogues, making traffic metrics meaningless — IndiaMART will DROP traffic as a reported metric. Opportunity: AI voice bots handling 10-20K calls/day (target 1 lakh/day), AI improving RFQ quality from 50-60% to 80-90% with quantity + specifications. CEO believes the real moat is behavioral data (what suppliers actually sell, at what price, to whom) — that's "nowhere scrapable." (Q3 concall p.6, Q2 concall p.7)

5. Margins Are Compressing (Intentionally) — EBITDA margin dropped from 38% (FY25) to 33% (Q2/Q3 FY26). This sounds bad, but the reasons are: (a) new ESOP grants of ₹90 Cr to retain employees, (b) performance marketing spend of ₹6 Cr/quarter to drive enquiry growth, (c) 6-9% headcount growth. These are investments, not competitive pressure. Importantly, the CEO himself guided for 33-35% margins and called the 38%+ margins "elevated because we are not adding customers." (Q3 FY25 concall p.8-9, Q3 FY26 concall p.12)

Investment Thesis

Bull Case (Why Buy)

  • Extreme valuation gap: P/E at all-time low of 19.6x while historical median is 48.9x. Earnings grew 55% last year. The market is pricing IndiaMART as a no-growth business — but revenue grew 13%, collections grew 15%, and deferred revenue grew 17%. Even at a modest 25x P/E (half of median), the stock would be ₹2,375 — a 20% upside from here.
  • ₹2,868 Cr net cash = built-in safety net: 24% of market cap is sitting in cash. Strip out the cash, and you're paying just ₹8,969 Cr for a business that generates ₹675 Cr cash annually. That's a 7.5% free cash flow yield — better than a fixed deposit, PLUS you own a growing business on top. The cash grows by ~₹150-200 Cr every quarter.
  • Busy Infotech is a hidden growth engine: Billing growing 28-57% YoY, deferred revenue ₹112 Cr (+56%), 12K new licenses per quarter. Currently contributes only ₹32 Cr/quarter revenue (8% of consolidated), but at this growth rate it could be ₹200+ Cr annual revenue in 2 years. IndiaMART's 220K paying SME customers are a captive distribution channel for accounting/ERP software. If Busy reaches ₹200 Cr revenue at 15% EBITDA margin, that alone adds ₹30 Cr to EBITDA.
  • Best-in-class business quality metrics: ROCE 34% (every ₹100 of capital generates ₹34 operating profit), negative working capital (-305 days means customers fund the business), zero debt (D/E 0.01), cash conversion better than net income (OCF/NI = 1.2x). These are characteristics of the highest quality businesses globally — subscription-based, capital-light, cash-generative.
  • Management credibility is rare: CEO who admits what he doesn't know, refuses to give unachievable guidance, drops metrics that become unreliable, and consistently delivers on what he commits to. In a market full of promotional management teams, this candor has a value that the market hasn't priced in.

Bear Case (Why Not)

  • Subscriber growth may be permanently stalled: 220K paying suppliers has been a ceiling for 6+ quarters. Net additions went from +2-3K to -1K (Q3 FY26). If this is structural (the addressable market of SMEs willing to pay ₹30K+/year is smaller than the 15-20M GST suppliers suggest), then revenue growth is capped at ARPU expansion of 6-8% per year. A company growing 6-8% doesn't deserve a 48x P/E — maybe 15-20x is fair, which is roughly where it trades today.
  • Silver churn is a ticking clock: ~7% monthly churn at the silver tier means ~50% of new silver customers leave within a year. Management has been working on this for 6+ quarters with "no significant change." Silver is the funnel that feeds gold and platinum tiers. If the funnel can't be replenished, even the healthy gold/platinum base (1% churn) will eventually erode. CEO acknowledged: "If we're not able to fix this in 2-3 quarters, we'll go back to ARPU-led growth at lower profitability."
  • AI disruption is real but hard to quantify: AI crawlers already made traffic metrics useless (company dropping the KPI). If AI makes it trivial for any platform to aggregate supplier catalogues, IndiaMART's listing moat weakens. The CEO argues behavioral data is defensible — but this argument needs continuous validation as AI capabilities improve quarterly.
  • Other income creates false earnings signal: Q1 FY26 other income was ₹92 Cr, Q2 was ₹10 Cr, Q3 was ₹135 Cr — all from the same ₹3,051 Cr treasury. This makes net profit lumpy and P/E misleading. On a normalized basis (using operating profit only), the business is less cheap than headlines suggest. Operating P/E is closer to 25x.
  • CEO is 56 years old with 49% stake: Succession risk. No obvious second-in-command who could replace Dinesh Agarwal's strategic thinking and candor. Brijesh Agrawal runs Busy well but the core IndiaMART vision is Dinesh's.

What to Watch — Key Triggers

  • Q4 FY26 subscriber adds (most important): Q4 is seasonally strong for renewals. If net adds go negative in Q4 too, it confirms structural stall. If they bounce back to +3-5K, it was just seasonality. This single data point will likely move the stock 10-15%.
  • Silver churn improvement: Management has been promising improvement "in 1-2 quarters" for over a year. Look for silver monthly churn dropping from 7% toward 5%. Even a 1 percentage point improvement would add ~2K net subscribers per quarter.
  • Busy Infotech new license growth: Currently 12K/quarter. If this accelerates to 15-20K, Busy becomes a material revenue contributor within 2 years. Watch deferred revenue trajectory — ₹112 Cr growing 56% YoY.
  • AI voice bot scale-up: Target is 1 lakh calls/day (from 10-20K today). If achieved, this could reduce customer service costs significantly and improve retention by resolving supplier issues faster. Watch for disclosure of actual cost savings.
  • Vertical strategy execution: CEO talked about hiring industry-specific people and building vertical expertise. If IndiaMART can show materially different unit economics in 2-3 focus verticals, it opens a new growth avenue.
  • Capital return announcement: Post-FY26 AGM (Jul-Aug 2026), watch for buyback or special dividend. With ₹3,000+ Cr cash and ₹600+ Cr annual CFO, a ₹200-300 Cr buyback at these valuations would be highly accretive.
